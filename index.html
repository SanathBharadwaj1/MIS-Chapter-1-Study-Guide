Chapter Overview

Chapter 1 introduces the big idea that information systems are not just about computers. They also involve people, communication, business processes, and the assumptions people bring into a system.

Self-Directed Learning Technology changes fast, so MIS students and professionals need to learn independently and adapt.
Mental Models People design and use systems based on their assumptions about how the world works.
Curse of Knowledge Experts often forget what it feels like to be a beginner, which makes communication harder.
Hype vs. Reality A new technology can get huge attention before it delivers practical value.

Vocabulary and Key Concepts

These definitions are written to be clear, precise, and test-ready.

Self-Directed Learning The process of learning independently by identifying what you need to know, finding trustworthy sources, and applying that knowledge to a problem. In MIS, this matters because technology changes quickly and many problems require people to teach themselves new tools.
Mental Model A person’s internal way of understanding how the world works. It includes beliefs, assumptions, and expectations shaped by experience, education, and culture.
Information System (IS) A combination of people, processes, data, software, and hardware used to collect, process, store, and communicate information.
Curse of Knowledge The tendency for people who already understand something to have difficulty imagining what it is like not to know it, which often leads to poor explanations.
Business Process A set of related activities an organization follows to complete a task or achieve a goal. In MIS, systems often automate or support these processes.
Technology Hype Cycle A model showing how expectations about a new technology rise quickly, exceed reality, fall into disappointment, and later recover as practical uses become clearer.
Peak of Inflated Expectations The stage of the hype cycle when excitement and expectations are unrealistically high.
Trough of Disillusionment The stage when enthusiasm drops because real results do not match the earlier hype.
Slope of Enlightenment The stage when people begin to understand where the technology creates real value.
Plateau of Productivity The stage when a technology becomes broadly useful because its benefits are more proven and stable.
Technology Adoption The decision by individuals or organizations to actually begin using a technology.
Diffusion The spread of a technology through a market or society over time.
Actual Demand Real demand shown by customers who are willing to buy, use, and continue using a product or service.
Perceived Market Readiness What firms think the market wants or is ready to adopt, whether or not customers actually behave that way.
Speculative or Anticipatory Adoption Investment or early use based on the belief that a technology will become more valuable in the future, even if current demand is still limited.
Best exam shortcut to remember: companies can spend heavily on a technology before it is widely adopted, and adoption can happen before full market diffusion.

Why These Ideas Matter in MIS

MIS is not just technical. It is about translating business needs into systems that people can actually use. If a designer’s mental model is wrong, the system may not match what users really need. If someone suffers from the curse of knowledge, they may explain the system poorly or design it in a way that only experts understand.

This chapter also helps explain why new technologies like AI can receive huge investment long before they become widely useful in everyday business settings. Spending shows interest and priorities, but usage shows whether the technology is actually creating value.

Chapter 1 Practice Quiz

1. Which statement best defines a mental model?
  • A. A hardware blueprint used to build computers
  • B. A person’s learned way of understanding how the world works
  • C. A dataset stored inside an information system
  • D. A chart showing business profits over time
Correct Answer: B
2. Why does the curse of knowledge matter in MIS?
  • A. It causes all new technologies to fail
  • B. It prevents hardware from processing data correctly
  • C. Experts may struggle to explain systems clearly to beginners
  • D. It guarantees that users will misunderstand software
Correct Answer: C
3. In the hype cycle, what usually happens during the trough of disillusionment?
  • A. A technology becomes fully mature and stable
  • B. Expectations collapse because results do not match the hype
  • C. Firms stop investing in all technology permanently
  • D. Real demand sharply exceeds supply for the first time
Correct Answer: B
4. Which of the following best explains why investment is not the same as adoption?
  • A. Companies can spend money on a technology even if customers do not use it widely
  • B. Adoption only matters for non-technical industries
  • C. Investment and adoption always occur at the same rate
  • D. Adoption refers only to employee salaries
Correct Answer: A
5. Which set correctly lists the five stages of the hype cycle in order?
  • A. Plateau of productivity, slope of enlightenment, innovation trigger, peak, trough
  • B. Innovation trigger, peak of inflated expectations, trough of disillusionment, slope of enlightenment, plateau of productivity
  • C. Peak of inflated expectations, innovation trigger, trough of disillusionment, diffusion, maturity
  • D. Actual demand, perceived demand, anticipatory demand, enlightenment, productivity
Correct Answer: B
