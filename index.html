Chapter 1 Study Guide – Setting the Stage

This page reviews core ideas from Chapter 1, including self-directed learning, mental models, the curse of knowledge, the hype cycle, and the relationship between technology investment and actual adoption.

Chapter Overview

Chapter 1 introduces the idea that information systems are not just about computers. They are also about people, business processes, communication, and the assumptions people bring into a system. The slides emphasize that technology is shaped by human thinking, and that misunderstanding often happens because experts forget what it is like to be beginners.

  • Self-directed learning is essential in technology fields.
  • Mental models affect how people design and use systems.
  • The curse of knowledge makes communication harder.
  • The hype cycle helps explain why technologies rise, disappoint, and mature.
  • Investment in technology is not the same as adoption or diffusion.

Vocabulary and Key Concepts

Self-Directed Learning The process of learning independently by identifying what you need to know, finding trustworthy sources, and applying that knowledge to a problem. In MIS, this matters because technology changes quickly and many problems require students and professionals to teach themselves new tools.
Mental Model A mental model is a person’s internal way of understanding how the world works. It can be thought of as a set of beliefs, rules, or assumptions shaped by background, education, culture, and experience. Mental models influence how people design systems, interpret information, and make decisions.
Information System (IS) An information system is a combination of people, processes, data, software, and hardware used to collect, process, store, and communicate information. A system is only as useful as the quality of the information it transmits and the people and processes behind it.
Curse of Knowledge The curse of knowledge is the tendency for people who already understand something to have difficulty imagining what it is like not to know it. This often makes experts poor communicators, because they skip steps, use jargon, or assume background knowledge that others do not have.
Business Process A business process is a set of related steps or activities that an organization follows to complete a task or achieve a goal. In MIS, programs often follow business processes, so flawed assumptions about a process can lead to flawed system design.
Technology Hype Cycle The hype cycle is a model describing how expectations about a new technology rise rapidly, often exceed reality, crash into disappointment, and then recover as practical uses become clearer. The classic stages are: innovation trigger, peak of inflated expectations, trough of disillusionment, slope of enlightenment, and plateau of productivity.
Peak of Inflated Expectations The stage in the hype cycle when excitement and expectations about a technology become unrealistically high. People may believe the technology will transform everything immediately, even before real evidence supports that view.
Trough of Disillusionment The stage in the hype cycle when enthusiasm falls because the technology does not yet meet the exaggerated expectations. Failures, limitations, and weak adoption become more visible at this stage.
Slope of Enlightenment The stage in which people begin to understand where the technology actually creates value. Instead of hype, the focus shifts to workable uses, better processes, and realistic results.
Plateau of Productivity The stage in which a technology becomes broadly useful and accepted because its practical benefits are clearer and more consistently delivered.
Technology Adoption The decision by individuals or organizations to begin using a technology in practice. Adoption depends not only on excitement, but also on real demand, readiness, usefulness, and cost.
Diffusion Diffusion is the broader spread of a technology through a market, industry, or society over time. A technology may be adopted by some users without yet achieving widespread diffusion.
Actual Demand Real customer demand shown by people who are actually willing to buy, use, and continue using a product or service.
Perceived Market Readiness What firms think the market wants or is prepared to adopt, whether or not customers actually behave that way.
Speculative or Anticipatory Adoption Investment or early adoption based on the belief that a technology will become more valuable in the future, even if current demand is still limited.

Why These Ideas Matter in MIS

MIS is not only technical. It is also about translating business needs into systems that people can use. That means communication matters. If a designer’s mental model is wrong, the system may block what users actually need to do. If an expert suffers from the curse of knowledge, they may explain a system poorly or design it in a way that only makes sense to other experts.

The chapter also connects MIS to real business decisions about AI and emerging technologies. A company can spend billions of dollars on AI, but that does not guarantee widespread adoption or useful results. Looking at the numbers matters because spending shows priorities, but usage shows whether the technology is actually creating value.

Good exam idea to remember: investment ≠ adoption ≠ diffusion.

Chapter 1 Practice Quiz

1. Which statement best defines a mental model?
  • A. A hardware blueprint used to build computers
  • B. A person’s learned way of understanding how the world works
  • C. A dataset stored inside an information system
  • D. A chart showing business profits over time
Correct Answer: B
2. Why does the curse of knowledge matter in MIS?
  • A. It causes all new technologies to fail
  • B. It prevents hardware from processing data correctly
  • C. Experts may struggle to explain systems clearly to beginners
  • D. It guarantees that users will misunderstand software
Correct Answer: C
3. In the hype cycle, what usually happens during the trough of disillusionment?
  • A. A technology becomes fully mature and stable
  • B. Expectations collapse because results do not match the hype
  • C. Firms stop investing in all technology permanently
  • D. Real demand sharply exceeds supply for the first time
Correct Answer: B
4. Which of the following best explains why investment is not the same as adoption?
  • A. Companies can spend money on a technology even if customers do not use it widely
  • B. Adoption only matters for non-technical industries
  • C. Investment and adoption always occur at the same rate
  • D. Adoption refers only to employee salaries
Correct Answer: A
5. Which set correctly lists the five stages of the hype cycle in order?
  • A. Plateau of productivity, slope of enlightenment, innovation trigger, peak, trough
  • B. Innovation trigger, peak of inflated expectations, trough of disillusionment, slope of enlightenment, plateau of productivity
  • C. Peak of inflated expectations, innovation trigger, trough of disillusionment, diffusion, maturity
  • D. Actual demand, perceived demand, anticipatory demand, enlightenment, productivity
Correct Answer: B
